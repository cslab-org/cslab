Newton's method is an iterative optimization technique (for finding maxima or minima of functions). It has quadratic convergence guarantee compared to the gradient descent method whose convergence is linear. But Newton's method requires that our initial guess has to be near the optimum.

This article analyses Newton's method for finding the maximum of the gaussian like function \(f(x) = exp(-x^2/2)\). Clearly the maximum is at x=0.

We ask the question "how close our initial guess has to be so as to converge to 0"??

Basic Idea

Newton's method is derived from the taylor series expansion of a function at a point a. $$ f(x) \approx f(a) + (x-a) f^{'}(a) + \frac{1}{2}(x-a)^2 f^{"}(a) $$ Differentiating w.r.t. x and equating to 0, we get $$ x = a -\frac{f^{'}(a)}{f^{"}(a)} $$ This is the point which maximzes (or minimizes) the quadratic approximation to f(x) at point a. So we move to the new x, settig a=x. We iterate until convergence.

When a>1 or a<-1

If the initial point we picked is greater than 1 or less than -1, the updated point will be farther from 1 and it will move away from 0 to infinity or to negative infinity.
We see that the quadratic approximation is inverted (shown as green curve) and its minimum is farther away from a.

when \(\frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \) < |a| < 1

We see that the quadratic approximation is concave, but the maximum of the quadratic function is on the other side of 0 and its magnitude is greater than a. This will result in the newton's method to go farther and farther away from 0 failing to converge again.

When |a| < \(\frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \)

When \( a < \frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \), the updated point will be smaller than a and hence the procedure will converge to zero.
